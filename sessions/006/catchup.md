# Session 006 · 5-Minute Catch-Up

Justin, Ian, and Alec opened the hood on seven AI agents running inside their own companies right now: the ones that save time, save money, and make more money. For each one they showed what it does, why they built it, the size of company and industry it was built for, and enough of the how that you could go build your own version.

## The 5-minute catch-up

**The frame: agents ask for outcomes, not answers.** Alec set the terms up front. Most people use conversational AI, sitting in a browser asking ChatGPT or Claude for answers. An agent is the opposite: you ask it for an outcome and it takes action on your behalf, hooked into your email, your tools, sometimes your phone, with a specific role. Ian's tell was that he keeps calling his agents by name, Atlas, Saint, Saucy, because they act like teammates, not chatbots.

**Ian's Echo Check turns AI visibility into revenue.** Ian built an AEO/GEO/SEO tool with his OpenClaw "Atlas" after an SEO expert showed him a paid tool on a call. It started as a private "Pulse Check" for his own sites and became echocheck.app: plug in a URL, get an echo score, see your competitors, and see your site the way an LLM sees it (schema and text, no images or JavaScript). The proof was concrete. BrandSauce went from roughly 26 indexed pages to over 14,500, and after months of zero, it booked five demos in a week that all came from ChatGPT and all became clients.

**Justin's content engine makes a month of content in a day.** Not one big agent, a chain of specialists. It starts with a real phone call between the three partners, records the transcript, and hands it down the line: transcript to landing page to Eventbrite, Zoom page, newsletter, and Instagram, then the live session to YouTube, to a re-optimized web page, to shorts, to tool-directory pages, to the follow-up newsletter. A human stays in the loop for quality so it never ships slop. Justin's math: the same output would need three or four junior marketers, north of $200,000 in headcount.

**Alec's iMessage appointment setter wins on the color of the bubble.** The insight everyone wrote down: a green text reads as automated and gets ignored, a blue iMessage reads as human and gets replies. Alec's agent sends blue-bubble texts to leads who opted in but never booked, asks the qualifying questions, and pushes them to a booked appointment. His business is paid per appointment, so the agent both saves the cost of a person and books more. The through-line Justin added: speed to lead. Answer in seconds, not twelve hours, and you keep the lead instead of losing it to the next vendor.

**Ian's Saints Classical LMS started as a login page and became a platform.** His wife's classical school needed parent and teacher logins, and the project kept growing until Ian realized he was building a full learning-management system. He built the first version brainstorming with Claude and Claude Code, then put an OpenClaw ("Saint") on the server for the next iteration: teacher and parent views, houses, tuition, enrollment, a built-in email system, even Slack, with an iOS app coming in August. The quiet growth engine is a blog the agent writes 2 to 3 times a day under the pen name C.S. Lewis, now 350-plus posts, which pushed the school past Google's EEAT bar. Enrollment went from 95 students to over 230, roughly 2.5x.

**Justin's Mission Control replaced eight-plus tools with one.** Email, Asana, time tracking, billing, Google Docs, and client reporting were all disconnected, so Justin built a single in-house operations platform with 20 to 50 agents inside it. Every client request lands in one view. An AI reply drafts in Justin's own voice (trained on tens of thousands of emails he actually wrote), time logs against each task, and a monthly client report that used to take hours now generates in under a minute. His philosophy for the whole hour: be the architect. Most software is a house someone else designed; build the one you actually want to live in.

**Alec's cold email agent booked 112 opportunities in a week.** The simplest build of the day, and deliberately so: one agent, one job. It scrapes a prospect database with Apify (he showed an attorney lead scraper and pulled up Codex), enriches each record so the outreach proves real research, writes hyper-personalized emails, and sends them through Instantly without burning a personal inbox. Last seven days with one client: about 1,700 people, roughly 3,500 emails, 112 opportunities, at reply rates he says are impossible without AI.

**Ian's SaucyBot gives every employee their own agent.** The one he was most excited about. BrandSauce grew 5x in six months, from 5 to about 30 people, fully remote across Ecuador, Ukraine, the Philippines, and the US. So Ian built an interface that provisions a personal OpenClaw for each employee, walking them through Telegram and the BotFather with an API token and a pairing token. Everyone gets read-only access to the company but can only build in their own folder, so they can experiment hard without breaking anything. Real examples: a front-end coordinator's client-onboarding tracker, finance running profitability analysis, a designer's live Spanish-English translator page, and a store-builder's product catalog pages. His master bot, the Sauce Boss, pulls every employee's learnings into one "big brain" hive mind for the next hire. Alec framed it with Jensen Huang's line: every CEO needs an OpenClaw strategy, and almost none have one. Ian's has been running one in production for months.

**The takeaway: start with one, then compound.** Nobody built 50 agents on day one. Ian's advice, echoed by all three, was to start at square one, ask your agent if it can do the next thing, and grow it iteratively. The offer at the close was small and optional: a free 15-minute call to see if it's a fit to build a custom agent with the team. Ian's closing metaphor stuck: like Cesar Millan training dogs, you don't train the bot, you train the human to work with it.
