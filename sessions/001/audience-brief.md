# Webinar 001 — Audience Intelligence Brief

**Purpose of this file:** Steer the slide deck for Session 001 ("How marketers are actually using AI to grow their income," May 21, 2026, 1pm ET) based on the actual humans registered, not the imagined audience.

**How to use this:** Every slide should pass the test "does this speak to the room described below." If not, cut or reframe. Specific content recommendations are at the bottom.

## 2. The pain we're solving (their actual job pressure right now)

Read between the lines of the firmographics and you get this:

> **"I work at a 100-person SaaS company that just raised. The board expects us to triple pipeline. I have three people on my team and a CMO who keeps saying 'use AI.' I don't know what to actually do on Monday morning."**

That's the room. The deck should answer that question.

Three sub-pains underneath it:

1. **"My team is too small to run the plays we used to run."** — Lean post-Series-B marketing teams with enterprise-level revenue targets.
2. **"My CMO/CEO keeps asking what we're doing with AI and I don't have a real answer."** — Career exposure. The matched email subject line "Invite Only for Senior Marketers" + the "get promoted for being the AI-fluent one" angle is hitting this directly. Don't abandon it on stage.
3. **"I'm researching tools every week and none of them turn into pipeline."** — Tool fatigue. They've tried Jasper, Clay, Apollo, Smartlead, ChatGPT. They want to see one workflow run end-to-end, not 12 tools mentioned in passing.

## 3. Content priorities for the deck (opinionated, in order)

### Tier 1: Lead with these — they map to actual job pressure

**Topic A — Operating like a 12-person team with 4 people.**
Frame Alec's "3x revenue in 30 days" demo this way: not as "look what AI can do," but as "this is how a small team punches above its weight." The career-advancement promise lands hardest on this exact group. Show the workflow end-to-end, not the highlight reel.

Slide treatment: live workflow walkthrough, not a tools list. Screenshots over screenshares of dashboards. Name the specific output ("this generated 14 SQL-qualified meetings in 11 days").

**Topic B — LLM SEO / answer engine optimization (Ian's playbook).**
This is the highest-leverage topic for this room and the deck should give it real estate. Half the matched companies sell B2B products that prospects now research through ChatGPT and Perplexity. Cybersecurity, fintech, devtools — every category is being researched in LLMs right now. Almost none of these marketers have a strategy for it. This is "0 to 1 traffic" for a channel that didn't exist 18 months ago.

Slide treatment: show a before/after of a real company's LLM visibility. Walk through the 3-5 actions to take this quarter. Make it concrete enough that a Marketing Manager could pitch it to their VP on Friday.

**Topic C — Demand gen automation for small B2B teams.**
ABM workflows, intent routing, personalization at scale — built with AI instead of a $50K/year stack. Hits the "build internal tools, no code required" promise from the converting email.

Slide treatment: one full workflow, walked through. Tools named, prompts shown, outputs displayed. Not a survey of 10 approaches.

### Tier 2: Include but don't lead

**Topic D — Content production without sounding like AI.** Editorial workflow framing, not "blog with ChatGPT." Brand voice preservation is the angle.

**Topic E — Marketing ops and reporting automation.** Only 2-3 MarOps people in the room, but they become internal champions. Worth a Rapid Three question, not a full segment.

### Tier 3: Cut or skip

- Generic "what is an AI agent" explainer slides. This room is past that.
- Image and video generation tools. Wrong audience.
- DTC and Shopify war stories. Only 2 B2C people in the room.
- Enterprise-scale case studies (Salesforce, IBM, etc.). Mis-sized for the room.

## 4. The reframed debate

Current framing — "AI replaces vs. multiplies marketers" — is fine but abstract.

Sharper version for this specific room:

> **"In the next 12 months, the marketers who get promoted will be the ones who [X]."**

Have panelists argue different X's:

- **Side A:** The ones who deeply learn one AI workflow and become the company's internal expert.
- **Side B:** The ones who use AI to ship 10x output and let results speak.

That debate is about them, not about the industry. It also directly delivers on the highest-converting email promise (get promoted for being the AI-fluent one).

## 5. Open with an audience poll

**Recommended opening slide / poll question:**

> "What's your biggest constraint right now — pipeline, content velocity, or proving ROI?"

Why this works:

- Gives live data to steer the panel in real time
- Signals "this session is for you" in the first 30 seconds
- The result becomes a LinkedIn post and a follow-up email asset ("Here's what 50 senior marketers said their biggest constraint is in May 2026")
- Based on the audience profile, pipeline almost certainly wins. That alone is content.

## 6. Voice and copy rules for the deck

Pulled from established AI4NTP brand voice. These are non-negotiable for any text on slides:

- **No em dashes.** Hard rule. Use periods, parentheses, or restructure.
- **No insider jargon** without immediate definition. If a term gets used, define it on the same slide.
- **Punchy short sentences over long constructions.** Read it aloud. If you run out of breath, cut it.
- **Soften anything that sounds cocky.** Confident, not arrogant. "Here's what worked" beats "here's what every marketer should be doing."
- **"We're the translators, not the oracles."** This positioning line should appear once, prominently, probably on the opening or closing slide.
- **Editorial aesthetic, not AI startup aesthetic.** Serif headlines (Fraunces), clean body type (Söhne), JetBrains Mono for any metadata or data callouts. Signal-lime accent (#D4FF3A) used sparingly for emphasis only. Deep ink on warm paper, not white on black gradient.

When introducing the operators, lead with the specific result, not the resume:

- **Ian Kilpatrick:** "Runs a portfolio of companies — including a software business, a school, and an LLM SEO tool — with AI as his one-person team." (Not "partner at BrandSauce.")
- **Justin Novak (host):** "Sold his first company from his college dorm room. Helped scale multiple companies that went on to raise $50M+. Now finds world-class AI operators and shares everything live." (This is the converting bio from the email — reuse it verbatim.)

## 8. The Rapid Three (audience-tuned)

Recommended Rapid Three questions, tuned for this exact room:

1. **"What's the one AI workflow you actually use every week?"** (Operator-specific, no theory.)
2. **"What's a tool you bought, hyped to your team, and now never open?"** (Vulnerability and tool fatigue acknowledgment.)
3. **"If you had one hour with a Marketing Manager at a Series B company, what would you tell them to build first?"** (Direct gift to the median attendee.)

## 10. What we're explicitly NOT building

To keep the deck disciplined, do not include:

- A "history of AI" slide
- A "here's what's new this week in AI" slide (it ages instantly)
- More than three logos on any single slide
- Stock photography of robots, brains, or glowing blue circuits
- Any slide that exists primarily to look impressive vs. teach something specific

---

## 11. Slide count guidance

For a 45-minute panel with 3 speakers, the deck should be roughly 18-25 slides:

- Cold open + poll (2 slides)
- Speaker intros (3 slides, one per operator with their specific win)
- Topic A workflow walkthrough (4-5 slides)
- Topic B LLM SEO playbook (4-5 slides)
- Topic C demand gen automation (3-4 slides)
- The reframed debate slide (1 slide, framing only)
- Rapid Three (3 slides or one slide with all three)
- Bonus offer + close (2 slides)

Slides should be sparse. One idea per slide. The operators talk; the slides anchor.

---

## 12. Sanity check before shipping the deck

Before declaring the deck done, run these checks:

- [ ] Would a Marketing Manager at a 150-person Series B SaaS company leave knowing exactly what to do Monday?
- [ ] Did we name specific tools, prompts, and workflows, or did we stay abstract?
- [ ] Did we avoid every item in section 10?
- [ ] Are there fewer than 5 em dashes total across the deck? (Target: zero.)
- [ ] Does the deck speak to the doer (Manager), the proof-needer (Director/VP), and the budget-defender (C-Suite) at least once each?
- [ ] Is the "translators, not oracles" line present exactly once, prominently?

If any of these fail, revise before shipping.
