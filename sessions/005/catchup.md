# Episode 005 · 5-Minute Catch-Up

Three builders started with the same live audience brief: a bakery business. The room shaped it into three metro-specific builds, LA, New York, and Nashville, then watched Ian, Justin, and Alec each turn the idea into a working prototype with different AI stacks.

## The 5-minute catch-up

**The room chose the brief, then the builders split by instinct.** Attendees suggested everything from credit repair to product rental marketplaces, but baked goods kept coming up in chat, so Brett locked the prompt: build a bakery-style business. Alec took LA and went B2B, Justin took New York and turned it into a local marketplace, and Ian took Nashville and made the food-truck version feel like a Sunday morning brand.

**Justin built Milk and Cookies, a marketplace for farmers, bakers, and delivery.** He started in Figma and Midjourney, then used Wispr Flow to talk a plain-English product brief into Claude Code inside VS Code. The first pass was a wireframe, then he rebuilt the visual system in Claude Design and fed it back into VS Code. By the final demo, consumers could build a weekly cookie box, bakers could upload inventory, and drivers could accept deliveries.

**Alec built Crumb B2B, cookies that close deals.** His angle was not a consumer bakery, it was a sales and retention tool: send logo-printed cookies to prospects or clients, preview the box in real time, then place the order. He used FireShot to capture Crumbl as a reference, Claude and ChatGPT for ideation, Lovable for the app, Higgsfield for logo and image generation, and Gemini/Nano Banana to mock up logos on cookies.

**Ian built Sunday Crumb, a Nashville bakery truck with user-created pastries.** Ian ran ChatGPT alongside multiple OpenClaw agents connected to the same server, one focused on the brand kit and another pushing the site and ordering flow. His build let customers scan a QR code, invent a pastry, choose fillings and toppings, name the creation, and submit it to the truck. He also showed the design refinement loop: screenshots, plain-language critique, better imagery, and a second pass that felt less like a default AI template.

**The real takeaway was speed, not pastries.** The audience's vanity vote favored Alec's Crumb B2B, but every build proved the same point: a non-technical operator can now turn a rough idea into a testable prototype in under an hour. Alec put it plainly near the end: the risk is low now. You can test the idea, run outreach, get feedback, and learn the tools without spending six months or $50,000 to find out if anyone cares.
