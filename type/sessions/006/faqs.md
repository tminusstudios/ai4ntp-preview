# Session 006 FAQ

### What are the seven agents they showed?

In order: Echo Check (Ian, AI visibility), the content engine (Justin, a month of content in a day), the iMessage appointment setter (Alec), the Saints Classical LMS (Ian), Mission Control (Justin, an in-house operations platform), the cold-email lead-gen agent (Alec, Apify plus Instantly), and SaucyBot (Ian, a personal agent for every employee). Each one maps to the trifecta: save time, save money, or make more.

### What actually is an agent?

Alec's definition from the top of the session: with conversational AI you ask for answers, with an agent you ask for outcomes. An agent is hooked into your email, your tools, sometimes your phone, and takes action on your behalf inside a specific role. The tell all three hosts share is that they name their agents (Atlas, Saint, Saucy) and talk to them like teammates, because they do real work, not just chat.

### What is OpenClaw, and do I need to be technical to use it?

OpenClaw is the agent runtime Ian runs across his companies. Instead of typing questions into a browser, you set up an OpenClaw with roles and actions and it goes and does things, including building software and setting up other agents. On the "is it safe" question: Ian's answer was that if it is set up correctly by someone who knows what they are doing, employees get read-only access to the company and can only build inside their own folder, so they can experiment hard without breaking anything. Justin's take: the setup can be technical and there are real security considerations, which is exactly why you want it configured by someone who has done it before.

### How would you set up an OpenClaw for my company? (asked live)

Justin put this question to Ian on stage. Ian's answer: start by talking to whoever runs the company or owns the space you want to improve, set that person up as the manager, and use a setup very similar to SaucyBot so they can provision bots and walk their team through it. The hard part is not the tech, it is showing people what is possible. Ian runs weekly calls with his own team for exactly that, then surfaces the best examples so ideas spread. His metaphor: like Cesar Millan training dogs, you do not train the bot, you train the human to work with it.

### 20 to 50 agents sounds like a lot. Where do I actually start?

Nobody built their stack in one go. Ian's advice, echoed by all three: start at square one and grow it iteratively. Pick one job you keep doing that is robotic and time-consuming, ask your agent if it can handle it, and build from there. Justin's Mission Control and content engine both started small and compounded. The point is to get one agent working, not to design the whole system up front.

### Which agent should I build first?

Whatever removes your biggest, most repetitive time sink. When Alec asked the room which agent resonated most, the answers were telling: one attendee working in marketing pointed to the content and social-posting engine, another to lead generation, and another liked the idea of many small, customized agents. Justin's favorite was the one that is hyper-specific and "swims in its own lane" rather than trying to do everything. Start narrow.

### What is AEO and GEO, and does it really move revenue?

AEO (Answer Engine Optimization) means putting clear FAQs on your site so AIs can find and reuse your answers. GEO (Generative Engine Optimization) means optimizing for the AI summary at the top of a search result. Ian showed the payoff: BrandSauce went from roughly 26 indexed pages to over 14,500, and after months of zero, booked five demos in a week that all came from ChatGPT and all became clients. Echo Check ([echocheck.app](https://echocheck.app)) is the free tool he built to measure and improve it.

### Do the hosts write the code themselves, or does AI do it?

It is agentic. Ian built the first Saints Classical LMS by brainstorming with Claude and Claude Code, then put an OpenClaw on the server to build the next version. Justin's content engine and Mission Control are chains of specialized agents he built and refined over time, with a human kept in the loop for quality so nothing ships as slop. The recurring lesson: give the agent a rich, real input (a transcript, a database, a screenshot) and it produces far better output than asking it to fill in blanks.
